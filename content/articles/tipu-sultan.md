Tipu Sultan ruled Mysore during a period of intense conflict with the British East India Company and its allies. He inherited a strong military state from Haidar Ali and continued efforts to resist British expansion.

His rule included diplomatic outreach, military innovation, revenue measures, and attempts to strengthen Mysore's economy. His death at Srirangapatna in 1799 marked a turning point in British power in South India.

Tipu's Mysore was one of the strongest Indian states confronting British expansion in the late eighteenth century. It inherited military reforms, centralized authority, and a strategic position from the earlier rule of Haidar Ali.

Tipu sought allies beyond India, including France and other powers hostile to Britain. This diplomacy reflected his understanding that the British East India Company was part of a wider imperial world.

He invested in rockets, fortifications, revenue administration, and state control over some commercial activities. These measures were meant to strengthen Mysore's ability to survive prolonged war.

The Fourth Anglo-Mysore War ended with Tipu's death in 1799. Mysore was reduced and reorganized, and British confidence in South India grew sharply after his defeat.

Tipu remains a debated figure because his career sits at the intersection of anti-colonial resistance, regional state-building, religious politics, and military modernization. Different communities remember him in different ways.

Historically, his importance lies in the seriousness of the challenge he posed to the Company. Mysore under Tipu was not a passive victim of colonial expansion; it was an organized state actively seeking ways to survive and compete.
