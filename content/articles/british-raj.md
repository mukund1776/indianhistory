The British Raj began after the end of East India Company rule in 1858. Crown rule brought railways, universities, legal institutions, census operations, and centralized administration.

It also intensified economic extraction, racial hierarchy, political control, and social disruption. Indian nationalism grew through petitions, reform movements, local struggles, and mass campaigns, eventually making British rule untenable.

The Raj presented itself as orderly and modern, but its institutions served imperial priorities. Railways, telegraphs, and law courts connected regions while also making extraction, surveillance, and military movement more efficient.

Education created new professions and publics. Lawyers, journalists, teachers, and reformers used print, associations, and political meetings to debate rights, representation, social reform, and national identity.

Nationalism did not develop in one straight line. Moderates, revolutionaries, social reformers, peasants, workers, religious organizations, and regional leaders all shaped the movement in different ways.

By the twentieth century, mass politics transformed the struggle. Gandhi's campaigns, labor actions, peasant protests, constitutional negotiations, and wartime pressures all contributed to the collapse of British authority.

The Raj also changed everyday life unevenly. Some Indians gained access to English education, professional employment, and new public institutions, while many rural communities faced debt, land pressure, famine, and the instability of cash-crop economies. Modern infrastructure did not automatically mean equal development.

Its legacy remains visible in parliamentary institutions, legal codes, railways, bureaucracy, and the English language. At the same time, the memory of colonial rule is inseparable from exploitation, racial hierarchy, political repression, and the long struggle to recover sovereignty.

Understanding the Raj therefore requires holding two facts together: it built institutions that outlived empire, and it built them within a system designed to keep India subordinate. That contradiction shaped the politics of independence.
