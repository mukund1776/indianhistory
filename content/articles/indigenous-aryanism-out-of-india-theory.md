Indigenous Aryanism, often linked with the Out of India Theory, argues that the Vedic Aryans were native to the Indian subcontinent rather than intrusive migrants from Central Asia or the steppe. In this view, the Rigvedic world, the Sindhu-Saraswati region, and the Harappan civilization belong to one long civilizational continuum.

Proponents begin with the absence of a clear archaeological break in northwestern India. They argue that the material record does not show a large incoming population replacing an older civilization. Instead, urban decline, settlement shifts, and regional cultural change can be explained through environmental stress, river movement, internal transformation, and changing economic networks.

The Sarasvati argument is central. The Rigveda describes Sarasvati as a powerful river. Indigenous Aryan writers identify it with the Ghaggar-Hakra system, which many connect with the dry river channels of the northwestern subcontinent. If the Rigvedic Sarasvati reflects a living river before its major decline, proponents argue that the Rigveda must preserve memories from a period earlier than commonly assigned in migration models.

The Harappan-Vedic identification is another major claim. Supporters argue that the Indus or Sindhu-Saraswati civilization should not be treated as culturally separate from early Vedic society. They point to continuities in geography, settlement, ritual symbolism, craft traditions, and later religious memory as signs that the Vedic people emerged from the same civilizational zone rather than arriving after its decline.

Language is treated differently in the Out of India model. Instead of seeing Indo-Aryan speech as entering South Asia, proponents argue that Indo-European languages expanded outward from a northern Indian homeland or from a broader Indo-Saraswati cultural sphere. Westward movements would then explain Indo-Iranian links, Mitanni Indo-Aryan terms, and wider Indo-European connections outside India.

Shrikant G. Talageri's reading of the Rigveda is often used to support this chronology. Talageri argues that the internal geography, clan lineages, river references, and sequence of Rigvedic books point to an Indian homeland and an east-to-west movement of Indo-European groups, rather than a migration into India. In this argument, the Rigveda is not a late memory of incoming pastoralists but a layered record of communities already rooted in the Sarasvati-Sindhu region.

Supporters also cite proposed decipherments of the Indus script when arguing that Harappan culture was Vedic or Indo-Aryan. Jijith Nadumuri Ravi and the Yajnadevam project present the inscriptions as readable through a Vedic/Sanskritic framework, treating seal texts as evidence for cultural and linguistic continuity between the Harappan world and the Rigvedic tradition. For proponents, this would close one of the largest gaps in the debate: it would make the urban Sindhu-Saraswati civilization legible within the same cultural world as early Vedic literature.

The decipherment claim remains a proposal rather than a settled scholarly consensus, but within the pro-Indigenous Aryan case it functions as an important supporting argument. If the Indus signs can be read in a Vedic or Indo-Aryan way, then the Harappan-Vedic connection becomes much stronger than an argument from geography or cultural continuity alone.

Proponents also challenge the older invasion framing. They argue that the nineteenth-century Aryan invasion model was shaped by colonial assumptions about race, conquest, and civilizational hierarchy. Even though many scholars now describe migration rather than invasion, Indigenous Aryan writers often hold that the old framing still influences how evidence is interpreted.

The pro-Indigenous Aryan case therefore rests on a combined reading of texts, rivers, archaeology, and historical memory. It presents Vedic culture as indigenous, the Harappan world as part of the same broad tradition, and later dispersals as moving outward from India rather than into it.
