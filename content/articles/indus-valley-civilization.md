The Indus Valley Civilization (also called the Harappan Civilization) developed sophisticated cities such as Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, with planned streets, drainage systems, and standardized weights.

Archaeological evidence suggests extensive trade networks and a script that remains undeciphered. The civilization declined around 1900 BCE, possibly due to climate change and shifts in river courses.

Its cities were carefully organized. Streets often followed planned grids, houses used standardized baked bricks, and drainage channels carried wastewater away from residential areas. This level of urban management suggests strong civic traditions, even if the exact political structure remains uncertain.

Harappan craft production was highly developed. Bead makers, metal workers, potters, and seal carvers produced goods that circulated across the Indus and Saraswati regions and beyond. Trade links connected these cities with regions such as Oman, Mesopotamia, and Central Asia.

The civilization's seals, weights, and measures point to a shared commercial and cultural system. The script has not yet been deciphered, so historians must rely on archaeology rather than royal inscriptions or literary accounts. This makes the Indus world both rich and difficult to interpret.

After the urban decline, many settlements became smaller or shifted location. The cultural tradition did not simply disappear; craft practices, rural settlements, religious symbols, and regional continuities likely carried aspects of this world into later phases of Indian history.

The relationship between the Indus-Saraswati world and later Vedic culture remains debated. Some interpretations emphasize continuity in settlement, ritual symbols, and ecology, while others stress linguistic and cultural change. The evidence is complex and still developing.

What is clear is that this civilization was one of the great early urban traditions of the ancient world. Its achievement lies not in giant royal monuments, but in civic order, craft standardization, water management, and a shared material culture across a vast region.
