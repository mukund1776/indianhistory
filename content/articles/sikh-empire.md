The Sikh Empire emerged under Maharaja Ranjit Singh, who unified competing Sikh misls and built a strong state centered on Punjab. Its territories extended into areas of present-day Pakistan, Kashmir, and the northwest.

The empire maintained a modernized army, employed officers from different backgrounds, and practiced a broad style of governance. It ended after the Anglo-Sikh Wars, when Punjab was annexed by the British.

Before Ranjit Singh, Sikh power was organized through misls, or confederacies, that emerged in the turbulent eighteenth century. He gradually brought these groups under a more centralized authority.

Lahore became the center of his kingdom. The state drew on Sikh, Punjabi, Persianate, and military traditions, while including Muslims, Hindus, Sikhs, and Europeans in different roles of administration and army service.

The army was one of the empire's great strengths. Infantry, artillery, cavalry, and European-trained units gave the Sikh state unusual military capacity in the northwest.

After Ranjit Singh's death, succession struggles weakened the kingdom. British expansion and the Anglo-Sikh Wars brought the empire to an end, but its memory remains central to the history of Punjab and Sikh political identity.

The Sikh Empire controlled a strategic frontier zone linking North India, Afghanistan, Kashmir, and Central Asia. That position gave it military importance and made diplomacy essential.

Its achievements are also social and cultural. Lahore became a major center of patronage, and Sikh sacred institutions gained protection and resources. The empire gave political expression to communities that had endured decades of persecution and warfare.

The Sikh Empire also stands out because it remained independent while the East India Company was expanding elsewhere. Its fall marked one of the final major steps in the British conquest of the subcontinent.
