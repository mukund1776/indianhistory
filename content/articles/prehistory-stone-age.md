Indian prehistory stretches across an immense span of time, from early stone-tool makers to communities that began farming, herding, and living in permanent settlements.

The subcontinent preserves evidence of handaxes, microliths, rock shelters, seasonal camps, and early village life. These developments did not create a single kingdom or empire, but they formed the human, technological, and ecological background from which later civilizations emerged.

The earliest phases are associated with large stone tools such as handaxes and cleavers. These objects show that early human groups understood stone quality, tool shape, and landscape use. Sites in river valleys, uplands, and rock shelters suggest that communities moved through varied environments rather than living in one fixed ecological zone.

Later prehistoric communities used smaller and more specialized tools. Microliths could be fitted into wood or bone to make composite tools useful for hunting, cutting, and processing plants. Rock art and repeated occupation of shelters point to growing symbolic life and deeper knowledge of local animals, seasons, and routes.

The shift toward farming and herding was gradual. At places such as Mehrgarh and other early village sites, people cultivated crops, domesticated animals, built mud structures, and exchanged goods. These changes created denser settlements, new food systems, and craft traditions that prepared the ground for the later urban worlds of the northwest.

Prehistory matters because it reminds us that Indian history did not begin with kings, inscriptions, or cities. Long before written records, communities were adapting to climate, rivers, stone, animals, and plants. Their choices shaped the basic patterns of settlement and survival that later civilizations inherited.
