The British East India Company began as a trading corporation but gradually became a territorial power. Victories at Plassey and Buxar, revenue rights in Bengal, and political intervention gave it enormous influence.

Company rule expanded through war, alliances, annexation, and administrative restructuring. The revolt of 1857 ended Company rule and transferred authority to the British Crown.

The Company's rise was not inevitable. It depended on Indian political fragmentation, military financing, alliances with local elites, and the ability to turn commercial privileges into territorial authority.

After gaining revenue rights in Bengal, the Company became both merchant and ruler. This created a new kind of power, one that extracted revenue, maintained armies, regulated trade, and intervened in succession disputes.

Company policies varied by region. Some areas were annexed directly, others were controlled through subsidiary alliances, and many princely states remained formally independent while dependent on British power.

The Company's rule produced deep economic and social consequences. Revenue demands, legal changes, military recruitment, and commercial priorities altered rural life, elite politics, and patterns of resistance.

The Company also transformed warfare in India. It recruited large Indian armies, used European drill and artillery, and financed campaigns with Indian revenue. This allowed a private corporation to defeat or subordinate older states.

By the nineteenth century, many Indians experienced Company rule through courts, collectors, soldiers, merchants, and tax demands rather than through a distant king. That everyday reach made the Company one of the most unusual and consequential political powers in Indian history.

Its history is a warning about how commercial power can become political power. The Company's armies, shareholders, officials, and Indian intermediaries together created an empire before Britain formally ruled India.
